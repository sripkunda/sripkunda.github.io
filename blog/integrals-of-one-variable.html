Integrals of One Variable

This article discusses the concepts of integration in one variable, focusing on both the mathematical intuition as well as the process of computation. Proofs that directly help with better understanding the concept (in my opinion) are presented along with the material. This is very brief and does not discuss reimann integrability, lebesgue integrals, methods of symbolically computing integrals, and many other things that may be generally covered in a first or second course in real analysis.

If this is your first encounter with integrals, you will likely have a hard time. This will be most beneficial to students who are interested in expanding their understanding after taking a more computation-focused course like Calculus II or AP Calculus.

Understanding Integrals

To come to the definition of a definite integral, we will first look at a technique used to find the approximation of the area underneath a function. This is called the Reimann sum. The idea is that we can use multiple side-by-side rectangles of certain widths to get a close approximation of our area.

Reimann Sums (Left, Right, and Midpoint Rules)

Mathematically, we can define a Reimann sum AAA of nnn subintervals with arbitrary points a<x1<⋯<xn−1<ba < x_1 < \dots < x_{n-1} < ba<x1​<⋯<xn−1​<b, the distances x1−a,x2−x1,…,b−xn−1x_1 - a, x_2 - x_1, \dots, b - x_{n-1}x1​−a,x2​−x1​,…,b−xn−1​ denoted Δx1,…Δxn\Delta x_1, \dots \Delta x_nΔx1​,…Δxn​. If we say that xk∗∈[xk−1,xk]x_k^* \in [x_{k-1}, x_k]xk∗​∈[xk−1​,xk​], then:

A=∑k=1nf(xk∗)ΔxkA = \sum^n_{k=1} f(x_k^*)\Delta x_kA=k=1∑n​f(xk∗​)Δxk​

We can get different values from the summation by altering our selections of xk∗x_k^*xk∗​ and the distances between each subinterval.

There are different "types" of Reimann sums. The left Reimann sum says that the left side of each of our rectangles intersects the function at a point. The right sum is the opposite, where the right side intersects the function. The midpoint sum says that we take the center of the width of each of our rectangles (the width being the horizontal side that lies on the xxx axis) as the intersection point instead.

Using this, we can create mathematical definitions of our reimann sums.

Left Sum

Our left sum will be an underestimation of the area if our function is monotonically increasing (increasing forever) and an overestimation if the function is monotonically decreasing (decreasing forever). The left sum is the specific case when we take the lowest value of xk∗x_k^*xk∗​ in our summation:

xk∗=xk−1x_k^* = x_{k-1}xk∗​=xk−1​
L(n)=∑k=1nf(xk−1)ΔxkL(n) = \sum^n_{k=1} f(x_{k-1})\Delta x_kL(n)=k=1∑n​f(xk−1​)Δxk​

Right Sum

Our right sum will be an overestimation of the area if our function is monotonically increasing and an underestimation if the function is monotonically decreasing. The right sum is the specific case when we take the highest value of xk∗x_k^*xk∗​ in our summation:

xk∗=xkx_k^* = x_{k}xk∗​=xk​
R(n)=∑k=1nf(xk)ΔxkR(n) = \sum^n_{k=1} f(x_k)\Delta x_kR(n)=k=1∑n​f(xk​)Δxk​

Middle Sum

The middle sum is the specific case when we take the average of the lowest and highest possible xk∗x_k^*xk∗​ values:

xk∗=xk−1−xk2x_k^* = \frac{x_{k-1} - x_k}{2}xk∗​=2xk−1​−xk​​
M(n)=∑k=1nf(xk−1−xk2)ΔxkM(n) = \sum^n_{k=1} f\left(\frac{x_{k-1} - x_k}{2}\right)\Delta x_kM(n)=k=1∑n​f(2xk−1​−xk​​)Δxk​

Defining Integration

We can now proceed with defining indefinite and definite integration.

Note that there are other forms of definite integration out there. For example, see Lebesgue Integrals, which allow us to extend integrals to a higher class of functions.

Definite Integrals

From our Reimann Sum, we can say that if we have an infinite amount of rectangles, we make the distances between each partition very tiny. With this, we can successfully find the exact area underneath the curve of our function. So, let's take a look at this mathematically.

From our reimann sum, we can say that the exact area under our curve is equal to:

lim⁡n→∞∑k=1nf(xk∗)Δxk=∫abf(x) dx\lim_{n \to \infty}\sum^n_{k=1} f(x_k^*)\Delta x_k = \int_a^b f(x) \, dxn→∞lim​k=1∑n​f(xk∗​)Δxk​=∫ab​f(x)dx

The right hand side of that equation is called a definite integral, and the left hand side is the definition of a definite integral as a Reimann sum. To be specific, this is the definition of a Reimann integral.

To clear up some confusion, integrating over the closed interval [a,b][a, b][a,b] is equivalent to integrating over (a,b)(a, b)(a,b). This is because we are infinitely close to aaa and bbb in our approximations anyways.

Indefinite Integrals

Indefinite integrals are a bit different from what we have seen as the "area under the curve." One way that I like to use to think of indefinite integrals is that when integrating a function fff, we are looking for some other function FFF such that F′=fF' = fF′=f. This is why we call the indefinite integral an "antiderivative," meaning:

ddx∫f(x) dx=f(x)\frac{d}{dx}\int f(x) \, dx = f(x)dxd​∫f(x)dx=f(x)

We will discuss the process of finding FFF (computing indefinite integrals) in the next section.

One thing worth mentioning is that there are actually multiple possible functions FFF such that F′=fF' = fF′=f, and they differ by a constant. This is because, for example:

ddx(x+1)=ddx(x+2)=ddx(x+c)=1\frac{d}{dx} (x + 1) = \frac{d}{dx} (x + 2) = \frac{d}{dx} (x + c) = 1dxd​(x+1)=dxd​(x+2)=dxd​(x+c)=1

Here, the equivalence remains true for all constants ccc. To show this in the value of our antiderivative, we add a constant of integration, usually denoted CCC, to FFF

Another way to say "indefinite integral" is "primitive."

To clarify, a function FFF such that F′=fF' = fF′=f is called a primitive of fff.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The fundamental theorem of calculus makes two main assertions and both involve integration. The second part is sometimes referred to as the "second fundamental theorem of calculus."

The first part says that if we have a function defined on the interval [a,b][a, b][a,b], and another function FFF defined on [a,b][a, b][a,b] as

F(x)=∫axf(t) dtF(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dtF(x)=∫ax​f(t)dt

Then, FFF is uniformly continuous on [a,b][a, b][a,b] and

F′(x)=f(x)F'(x) = f(x)F′(x)=f(x)

In other words, ∫axf(t) dt\int_a^x f(t) \, dt∫ax​f(t)dt, or FFF is another primitive of FFF.

Uniform continuity, as opposed to regular continuity, implies that the the change in the function (how the function turns) is also limited. You can learn more about this on the wikipedia page.

The sections below present a proof of both parts of the fundamental theorem. Please note that I did not write these proofs. I wrote and adapted an existing (and relatively common) proof for use in this post.

A Corollary of Part 1

From the first part, we can observe an interesting corollary that emerges if we do some rearranging.

We know that two primitives differ only by a constant CCC, which we usually refer to as the constant of integration. This means that:

F(x)−∫axf(t) dt=CF(x) - \int_a^x f(t) \, dt = CF(x)−∫ax​f(t)dt=C

Recall that part one proved that ∫axf(t) dt\int_a^x f(t) \, dt∫ax​f(t)dt is a primitive.

If we set x=ax = ax=a, then:

F(a)−∫axf(t) dt =F(a)=CF(a) - \int_a^x f(t) \, dt\ = F(a) = C F(a)−∫ax​f(t)dt =F(a)=C

Now,

F(x)−∫axf(t) dt=F(a)F(x) - \int_a^x f(t) \, dt = F(a)F(x)−∫ax​f(t)dt=F(a)

With some rearranging,

F(x)−F(a)=∫axf(t) dtF(x) - F(a) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dtF(x)−F(a)=∫ax​f(t)dt

After setting b=ab = ab=a,

∫abf(t) dt=F(b)−F(a)(3)\int_a^b f(t) \, dt = F(b) - F(a) \tag{3} \label{3}∫ab​f(t)dt=F(b)−F(a)(3)

This gives us an interesting relationship between the primitive and the computation of a definite integral.

Mean Value Theorem for Integrals

The Mean Value Theorem that you may have studied in differential calculus can also be applied to primitives. If FFF is a primitive of a function fff defined on [a,b][a, b][a,b], then there exists a value of ccc such that:

F′(c)=F(b)−F(a)b−aF'(c) = \frac{F(b) - F(a)}{b - a}F′(c)=b−aF(b)−F(a)​

Because FFF is a primitive of fff, F′(c)=f(c)F'(c) = f(c)F′(c)=f(c):

f(c)=F(b)−F(a)b−af(c) = \frac{F(b) - F(a)}{b - a}f(c)=b−aF(b)−F(a)​
f(c)(b−a)=F(b)−F(a)=∫abf(x) dxf(c)(b - a) = F(b) - F(a) = \int_a^b f(x) \, dxf(c)(b−a)=F(b)−F(a)=∫ab​f(x)dx

Therefore, there exists a value of ccc such that:

∫abf(x) dx=f(c)(b−a)\int_a^b f(x) \, dx = f(c)(b - a)∫ab​f(x)dx=f(c)(b−a)

Basic Properties of Primitives

Before jumping into the techniques for computation, it is useful to first familiarize ourselves with the mathematical properties of integrals. You can usually convince yourself of most of these properties as they come directly from differentiation.

  1. ∫f′(x) dx=f(x)+C\int f'(x) \, dx = f(x) + C∫f′(x)dx=f(x)+C (by definition)

  2. ∫f(x)±g(x) dx=∫f(x) dx±∫g(x) dx\int f(x) \pm g(x) \, dx = \int f(x) \,dx \pm \int g(x) \, dx∫f(x)±g(x)dx=∫f(x)dx±∫g(x)dx

These will be very useful in proofs, computations, and applications.

Substitutions

Substitutions involve the decomposition of larger integrals so that we can bring it down to an integral that we can already compute. It is sometimes referred to as a "reverse" of the chain rule of differentiation. Let's try the following integral.

∫x5x4+1 dx\int \frac{x^5}{x^4+1} \, dx∫x4+1x5​dx

This is a bit of a tricky substitution as it is not so obvious. First, let's separate the most "obvious" elementary function:

∫x5x4+1 dx=∫x dx−∫xx4+1 dx\int \frac{x^5}{x^4+1} \, dx = \int x \, dx - \int \frac{x}{x^4+1} \, dx∫x4+1x5​dx=∫xdx−∫x4+1x​dx

Note that rewriting rational expressions of polynomials (in this case to simplify the integral) is called Partial Fractions. You can read more about it here as we don't cover it in this article. Usually this can be recognized and done with enough algebraic manipulation.

Evaluating the first integral, we get:

12x2−∫xx4+1 dx+C\frac 1 2 x^2 - \int \frac{x}{x^4+1} \, dx + C21​x2−∫x4+1x​dx+C

Now, we can apply our substituion. Let u=x2u = x^2u=x2, making du=2x dxdu = 2x \, dxdu=2xdx. From here, we can substitute uuu to bring us to yet another one of our common integrals:

Changing of Bounds for Definite Integrals

You may notice at some point that, involving substitutions, the bounds of definite integrals will change. Take, for example, the the integral:

∫−π2π23sin⁡(x2)−5cos⁡(π−x) dx\int^{\frac{\pi}{2}}_{-\frac{\pi}{2}} 3\sin\left(\frac x 2\right) - 5 \cos(\pi - x) \, dx∫−2π​2π​​3sin(2x​)−5cos(π−x)dx

∫3sin⁡(x2)−5cos⁡(π−x) dx=3∫sin⁡(x2) dx−5∫cos⁡(π−x) dx\int 3\sin\left(\frac x 2\right) - 5 \cos(\pi - x) \, dx = 3 \int \sin\left(\frac x 2\right) \, dx - 5 \int \cos(\pi - x) \, dx∫3sin(2x​)−5cos(π−x)dx=3∫sin(2x​)dx−5∫cos(π−x)dx

3∫sin⁡(x2) dx=6∫sin⁡(u) du=−6cos⁡(u)3 \int \sin\left(\frac x 2\right) \, dx = 6 \int \sin(u) \, du = -6\cos(u)3∫sin(2x​)dx=6∫sin(u)du=−6cos(u)

−5∫cos⁡(π−x) dx=5∫cos⁡(v) dv=5sin⁡(v)-5 \int \cos(\pi - x) \, dx = 5 \int \cos(v) \, dv = 5 \sin(v)−5∫cos(π−x)dx=5∫cos(v)dv=5sin(v)

Integration By Parts

We can derive the idea of integration by parts relatively easily, so let's do it. First, we start with the product rule of derivatives. Recall that:

(fg)′=f′g+g′f(fg)' = f'g + g'f(fg)′=f′g+g′f

Integrating, we receive:

fg=∫f′g+∫g′ffg = \int f'g + \int g'ffg=∫f′g+∫g′f

Let u=fu = fu=f and v=gv = gv=g. Subtracting ∫f′g\int f'g∫f′g on both sides:

∫fg′=fg−∫f′g\int fg' = fg - \int f'g∫fg′=fg−∫f′g

The formula above is the idea behind integration by parts. We want the left hand side of the equation to represent the integral that we are solving, so we must choose a ggg that is a primitive of a part of our original function (hopefully something that we can easily integrate).

Note that it does not matter what fff and ggg are, as the function names are interchangeable.

Let's jump into a rather tedious but explanatory problem.

∫(4x3−9x2+7x+3)e−x dx\int (4x^3-9x^2+7x+3)e^{-x} \, dx∫(4x3−9x2+7x+3)e−xdx

We can first decide what fff and ggg will be. Although (as mentioned before) the letters are interchangeable, it might be useful to keep everything consistent.

f=4x3−9x2+7x+3  ⟹  f′=12x2−18x+7f = 4x^3-9x^2+7x+3 \implies f' = 12x^2 - 18x + 7f=4x3−9x2+7x+3⟹f′=12x2−18x+7
g=−e−x  ⟹  g′=e−xg = -e^{-x} \implies g' = e^{-x}g=−e−x⟹g′=e−x

With this substitution:

∫(4x3−9x2+7x+3)e−x dx=−e−x(4x3−9x2+7x+3)+∫(12x2−18x+7)e−x dx\int (4x^3-9x^2+7x+3)e^{-x} \, dx = -e^{-x}(4x^3-9x^2+7x+3) + \int (12x^2-18x+7)e^{-x} \, dx∫(4x3−9x2+7x+3)e−xdx=−e−x(4x3−9x2+7x+3)+∫(12x2−18x+7)e−xdx

We can leave out the constants of integration for now because they can all be combined into a single constant at the end anyways.

Now, we perform a similar substitution again to evaluate ∫(12x2−18x+7)e−x dx\int (12x^2-18x+7)e^{-x} \, dx∫(12x2−18x+7)e−xdx

∫(12x2−18x+7)e−x dx=−e−x(12x2−18x+7)+∫(24x−18)e−x\int (12x^2-18x+7)e^{-x} \, dx = -e^{-x}(12x^2-18x+7) + \int (24x-18)e^{-x}∫(12x2−18x+7)e−xdx=−e−x(12x2−18x+7)+∫(24x−18)e−x

Again,

∫(24x−18)e−x=−e−x(24x−18)+24e−x\int (24x-18)e^{-x} = -e^{-x}(24x-18) + 24e^{-x}∫(24x−18)e−x=−e−x(24x−18)+24e−x

Substituting everything back in,

−e−x(4x3−9x2+7x+3)−e−x(12x2−18x+7)−e−x(24x−18)+24e−x-e^{-x}(4x^3-9x^2+7x+3) - e^{-x}(12x^2-18x+7) - e^{-x}(24x-18) + 24e^{-x}−e−x(4x3−9x2+7x+3)−e−x(12x2−18x+7)−e−x(24x−18)+24e−x
=−(4x3−9x2+7x+3+12x2−18x+7+24x−18+24)e−x= -(4x^3-9x^2+7x+3+12x^2-18x+7+24x-18+24)e^{-x}=−(4x3−9x2+7x+3+12x2−18x+7+24x−18+24)e−x
=−(4x3+3x2+13x+16)e−x= -(4x^3+3x^2+13x+16)e^{-x}=−(4x3+3x2+13x+16)e−x

This leaves us at our solution.

Parametric Functions

Parametrizing functions is a very useful method of representing curves. Integrating functions when given in this form is an equally essential technique.

The derivation of the method of integration used is relatively simple. A parametrization of a point in a two-dimensional space will resemble:

x=f(t)x = f(t)x=f(t)
y=g(t)y = g(t)y=g(t)

More naturally, xxx and yyy are coordinates of a point that varies with respect to a parameter ttt.

We will compute the integral of y=F(x)=F(f(t))y = F(x) = F(f(t))y=F(x)=F(f(t)):

∫F(x) dx\int F(x)\, dx∫F(x)dx

Assuming that xxx is a substitution, x=f(t)x = f(t)x=f(t) and dx=f′(t) dtdx = f'(t) \, dtdx=f′(t)dt. Substituting, we recieve:

∫y f′(t) dt=∫g(t) f′(t) dt\int y \, f'(t) \, dt = \int g(t) \, f'(t) \, dt∫yf′(t)dt=∫g(t)f′(t)dt
