Exploring Gradient Descent

Gradient Descent is a first-order optimization method in which we "descend" along the opposite direction of the gradient of our objective function to find its global minimum.

Variants of this method are widely used in training neural networks, which you can learn more about in this article.

In this article, we will explore the theory behind the gradient descent algorithm. We will analyze the convergence of the algorithm for certain types of functions and understand the limitations of the algorithm.

Introduction

The idea behind gradient descent is relatively intuitive given that we understand the notion of the "gradient" of a function. The primary concept to remember is that the gradient of some function f:Rn→Rf: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}f:Rn→R is a vector field of the partial derivatives fff.

∇f(x)=[∂f∂x1(x)⋮∂f∂xn(x)]\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{bmatrix}\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(\mathbf{x}) \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}(\mathbf{x})\end{bmatrix}∇f(x)=⎣⎡​∂x1​∂f​(x)⋮∂xn​∂f​(x)​⎦⎤​

Let's look at this from the concept of a directional derivative, where we project the gradient vector along some unit vector v\mathbf{v}v. This can be computed using a dot product:

∇vf=(∇f)⊤v=∥∇f∥∥v∥cos⁡θ\nabla_{\mathbf{v}} f = (\nabla f)^\top\mathbf{v} = \| \nabla f \| \| \mathbf{v}\|\cos\theta∇v​f=(∇f)⊤v=∥∇f∥∥v∥cosθ

where θ\thetaθ is the angle between ∇f\nabla f∇f and v\mathbf{v}v. Now, notice that this directional derivative is maximized when cos⁡θ=1\cos\theta = 1cosθ=1, or when v\mathbf{v}v is the unit vector of ∇f\nabla f∇f. In other words, the gradient vector at some point always points in the direction of the greatest increase.

Now we want to go to the minimum of our function. To do this, we would naturally go in the direction of the greatest decrease, which is the in the negative direction of our gradient vector.

From the idea of taking a step in the negative direction of the gradient, we can create a rule for updating our approximation of the local minimum. Each iteration of the algorithm is referred to as a "step," because we take a step in the direction of the negative gradient. We have an objective function f(x)f(\mathbf{x})f(x), an initial point x(0)\mathbf{x}^{(0)}x(0), and a set of learning rates denoted {η1,…,ηn}\{ \eta_1, \dots, \eta_n\}{η1​,…,ηn​} such that ηi>0\eta_i > 0ηi​>0. For i=0,…,ni = 0,\dots,ni=0,…,n:

x(i+1)=x(i)−ηi+1∇f(x(i))\mathbf{x}^{(i + 1)} = \mathbf{x}^{(i)} - \eta_{i + 1}\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^{(i)})x(i+1)=x(i)−ηi+1​∇f(x(i))

The learning rate is a parameter to our algorithm that is sometimes called the "step size." This defines how far along the gradient we travel for each iteration. Sometimes it will prove useful to set differing step sizes for each iteration of the algorithm.

Notice that for each iteration, we are traveling in the direction of the negative gradient of that point. We repeat this process for nnn iterations, and the idea is that the algorithm will (ideally) converge to a nice global minimum.
