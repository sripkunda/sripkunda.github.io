Linear Algebra Primer

This article is a fast paced introduction to Linear Algebra. You might benefit from reading it as a little preview to your linear algebra course, or as a review of topics that you have already studied.

Linear algebra is the study of linear relationships, linear maps, and their many representations. There are countless applications in various subjects in computer science, engineering, physics, and (naturally) mathematics. Linear algebra has been cleverly applied to other seemingly unrelated fields as well. To see the power of this subject, it may be worth looking into Google's PageRank Algorithm.

This treatment leaves out details for the sake of speed and understanding, as it is meant to be a quick introduction to the topic. The prerequisites are single variable calculus and set theory, although enough mathematical maturity should get you through most of the content. For a review of integration, see my writeup on integrals.

Many things I want to write about require linear algebra, so I wrote this primarily for people to use it as a reference. The content is based on the books Linear Algebra Done Right by S. Axler and Linear and Geometric Algebra by A. Macdonald.

Vectors

You may have been introduced to the idea behind a "vector" in a general math course or physics course. Often, vectors are considered quantities that contain both a "magnitude and a direction," or sometimes "lists of numbers." Both of these definitions may be a sufficient introduction to understanding how vectors are represented, but not necessarily what they are.

Linear algebra axiomatizes the idea of a vector by defining what is called a vector space. If you are not familiar with a "axiom-theorem-proof" style of mathematics, it may take some more time to grasp this material.

We can start by creating some definitions. Try to understand them, but we will talk about them afterwards.

Subspaces

A subpsace UUU of a vector space VVV is a nonempty subset of VVV that inherits scalar multiplication and vector addition from VVV. The following must be satisfied for u1,u2∈U\mathbf{u}_1, \mathbf{u}_2 \in Uu1​,u2​∈U:

  1. (u1+u2)∈U(\mathbf{u}_1 + \mathbf{u}_2) \in U(u1​+u2​)∈U (Closed under vector addition)
  2. au1∈Ua\mathbf{u}_1 \in Uau1​∈U (Closed under scalar multiplication)

Note that in order for UUU to be closed under scalar multiplication, the zero vector must also be in UUU.

A trivial example of a subspace would be R3\mathbb{R}^3R3, which is a subspace of Rn\mathbf{R}^nRn.
